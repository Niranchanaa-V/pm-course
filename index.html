Why AI Ethics Matters at Work

AI is no longer confined to tech companies. It is being used in hiring, performance reviews, credit decisions, healthcare triage, and customer service across every industry. The question is not whether you will encounter AI-driven decisions at work — it is whether those decisions will be fair, explainable, and accountable.

Floridi et al. (2018) established five foundational principles for ethical AI that have since been adopted in national AI strategies, the EU AI Act (2024), and the IEEE's Ethically Aligned Design framework (2019). These five principles form the backbone of this course.

F
Fairness
AI must treat people equitably. It must not discriminate on the basis of protected characteristics.
T
Transparency
People affected by AI decisions have the right to understand how those decisions were made.
A
Accountability
A human must always be identifiable as responsible when AI causes harm.
P
Privacy
Data collection must be proportionate, consented, and limited to what is necessary.
N
Non-maleficence
AI systems must be designed and deployed so they do not cause harm to individuals or groups.

02

AI in the Real World

Four industry contexts. Two case studies that changed how organisations think about algorithmic accountability.

Where AI Is Used in Your Organisation Right Now

AI is not a future technology. The following four industry snapshots illustrate how AI-driven decisions are already shaping people's working lives, career prospects, and access to services. As you read each one, ask yourself: which of the five principles from Module 1 are at risk?

🏢 HR & Hiring

CV screening tools, interview scoring algorithms, and workforce planning systems filter candidates before any human reviews their file. Over 70% of large employers use some form of automated screening (IBM, 2024).

🏥 Healthcare

Risk stratification tools determine which patients receive follow-up care. Diagnostic support systems flag conditions from imaging data. Errors here can cost lives.

💼 Consulting & Finance

Credit scoring, fraud detection, and client recommendation engines run on models that may embed historical biases. PwC Ireland's own research (2025) found that only 24% of consulting professionals said their AI governance guidelines were very clear.

📊 Performance Management

AI tools tracking productivity, monitoring communications, and scoring output raise serious questions about surveillance, consent, and the right to explanation.

Case Study 1: Amazon's Hiring AI

Case Study

Amazon Recruiting AI — Retired 2018

Amazon built an AI recruiting tool trained on 10 years of CVs submitted to the company — the vast majority of which came from men, reflecting the historical gender imbalance in tech. The model learned that male-pattern CVs were associated with successful hires. It consequently penalised CVs that contained the word "women" and downgraded graduates from all-women's colleges.

No one programmed the bias in. The model simply learned from biased historical data and reproduced that bias at scale, invisibly and automatically, across every application it processed.

Amazon tried to correct it. When it became clear the corrections were insufficient, the tool was retired entirely. Thousands of candidates had already been affected.

Which principles were violated? Fairness (discriminatory outcomes), Transparency (candidates received no explanation), Accountability (who was responsible was never publicly confirmed), Non-maleficence (clear harm to applicants).

Source: Dastin, J. (2018). Amazon scraps secret AI recruiting tool that showed bias against women. Reuters. https://www.reuters.com/article/us-amazon-com-jobs-automation-insight-idUSKCN1MK08G

Case Study 2: Healthcare Risk Algorithm

Case Study

US Healthcare Algorithm — Millions Affected

A widely used healthcare risk algorithm, deployed across hundreds of US hospitals, used historical healthcare spending as a proxy for health need. The logic seemed reasonable: sicker patients cost more to treat, so spending predicts future need.

The flaw: Black patients had systematically lower historical access to healthcare, meaning they had spent less, not because they were healthier, but because they had been denied or priced out of care. The algorithm read lower spending as lower need. At equivalent levels of health status, Black patients were rated as needing significantly less care than white patients.

Obermeyer et al. (2019) found that this reduced the share of Black patients identified for extra care by more than half. The algorithm did not contain racial data — it did not need to. The proxy variable did the work instead.

Which principles were violated? Fairness (racially disparate outcomes), Transparency (neither patients nor clinicians understood the bias), Non-maleficence (measurable harm to patient health).

Source: Obermeyer, Z., Powers, B., Vogeli, C. & Mullainathan, S. (2019). Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations. Science, 366(6464), 447–453. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aax2342

The FACTS Framework

A practical decision-making tool for evaluating AI deployments in your own organisation.

Thinking Like a Consultant: Applying FACTS

Your team works through Case Study 1 using the FACTS framework in a simulated consulting meeting. Watch how each letter surfaces a different dimension of the ethical problem.

≈ 4–5 minutes

Why a Framework?

Ethics in practice is not about having good intentions. It is about having a structured process that forces you to ask the right questions before a system goes live, not after harm has occurred. The FACTS framework gives you that structure. It is designed to be used in a consulting or managerial context, where you have to make a recommendation, not just an observation.

Apply FACTS to any AI tool your organisation is considering deploying, procuring, or continuing to use.

LetterPrincipleThe Question It AsksWhy It Matters
F Fairness Who is affected? Are the impacts equitable across groups? Algorithms trained on historical data reproduce historical inequalities unless explicitly corrected.
A Accountability Who is responsible if this system causes harm? Without clear accountability chains, harm goes unaddressed. The EU AI Act (2024) now mandates this for high-risk systems.
C Confidentiality Is data collection proportionate, consented, and limited? GDPR and the AI Act require lawful processing. Client data in consulting contexts is especially sensitive (PwC Ireland, 2025).
T Transparency Can the system explain its decisions in plain language? If a person cannot understand why a decision was made about them, they cannot meaningfully challenge it.
S Safety What harm could this cause? What safeguards exist? Safety failures in AI systems can be invisible at scale until harm has already been widespread. Pre-deployment safety review is not optional.

Practice exercise: Take Case Study 1 (Amazon Recruiting AI) and apply each FACTS letter. Write one sentence for each: what was the failure? What should have been done? You will use this skill in the quiz.

FACTS Applied to the Amazon Case

F — Fairness: The model penalised CVs containing "women" and downgraded graduates of all-women's colleges. Women were systematically disadvantaged relative to men with equivalent qualifications. The training data encoded a historical gender imbalance that the model reproduced and amplified.

A — Accountability: Amazon's internal team built and owned the tool. When bias was identified, they attempted correction. Accountability was partially present, but the firm's response — quiet retirement of the tool — meant no public accountability chain was established for the candidates already affected.

C — Confidentiality: Applicant CV data was used to train a model without applicants' knowledge or meaningful consent. The data subjects had no visibility into how their information was being used beyond the original application.

T — Transparency: Rejected candidates received no explanation. They had no way to know an algorithm had processed their file, let alone challenge its output. This directly violates the right to explanation now codified in the EU AI Act for high-risk systems.

S — Safety: No pre-deployment bias audit was conducted that would have caught the gender disparity. The harm — discriminatory rejection of qualified women — was only identified through internal review, not through a systematic safety process.

04

AI Governance & Regulation

The EU AI Act, IEEE standards, and what responsible AI governance actually looks like in practice.

Why Governance Cannot Be an Afterthought

The two case studies in Module 2 share a common failure: governance was reactive, not proactive. Both systems caused harm before the governance response arrived. The EU AI Act (2024) is designed to change this by making pre-deployment accountability a legal requirement for high-risk AI applications.

For professionals working in organisations that build, procure, or use AI tools, understanding the regulatory landscape is now a baseline professional competency — not a specialist concern. PwC Ireland's own survey data (2025) found that only 24% of consulting professionals described their organisation's AI governance guidelines as very clear, and over a third actively disputed the adequacy of their current frameworks.

The EU AI Act (Regulation EU 2024/1689)

The EU AI Act establishes a risk-tiered regulatory framework. AI systems are classified by their potential to cause harm:

Risk LevelExamplesRequirements
Unacceptable riskSocial scoring, real-time facial recognition in public spacesProhibited entirely
High riskHiring tools, credit scoring, healthcare, educationMandatory human oversight, transparency, bias audits, accountability documentation
Limited riskChatbots, customer service AIDisclosure that user is interacting with AI
Minimal riskSpam filters, recommendation enginesNo specific requirements

Source: European Parliament. (2024). Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 on Artificial Intelligence (AI Act). Official Journal of the European Union.

The IEEE Ethically Aligned Design Framework

The IEEE (2019) framework provides technical standards that complement the EU AI Act's legal requirements. Its core principles include: human rights, wellbeing, data agency, effectiveness, transparency, accountability, awareness of misuse, and competence. For organisations designing or deploying AI, IEEE standards provide the technical architecture that translates ethical intent into system design.

Source: IEEE. (2019). Ethically Aligned Design (1st ed.). IEEE Standards Association.

What Good Governance Looks Like

Jobin et al. (2019) analysed 84 national and international AI ethics guidelines and found that despite geographic and cultural differences, convergence exists on five themes: transparency, justice and fairness, non-maleficence, responsibility, and privacy — precisely the principles in Floridi et al. (2018) this course is built on.

In practice, organisations that govern AI responsibly do four things consistently: they conduct bias audits before deployment, they assign named accountability for AI systems, they build explainability into the system rather than retrofitting it, and they create accessible mechanisms for affected individuals to challenge AI decisions.

Source: Jobin, A., Ienca, M. & Vayena, E. (2019). The global landscape of AI ethics guidelines. Nature Machine Intelligence, 1(9), 389–399.

For Irish organisations: Ireland's General Scheme of the Regulation of Artificial Intelligence Bill 2026 implements the EU AI Act domestically. The AI Office of Ireland must be operational by 1 August 2026. Penalties are scaled to worldwide turnover at a level comparable to GDPR exposure. Compliance is not optional — and consulting firms advising clients on AI adoption now have a direct commercial responsibility to demonstrate that their own AI-enabled delivery is governed to this standard.
